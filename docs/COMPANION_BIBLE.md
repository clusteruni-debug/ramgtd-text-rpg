# The Abyss — Companion Bible v3.0

> Core question: "What does it take for a person to change?"
> Theme: **The real-world problems crushing Korea's 20s/30s/40s generation** — mapped onto an afterlife processing facility.
> All 10 companions are **the dead**. Their trauma at death made their data too heavy to process.
> Unlike the protagonist (who revives but loses memories), companions face **permanent death**.

---

## Protagonist's 10 Real Memories — Human Relationships

The protagonist is a burned-out Korean 30-something. Their problem was never work — it was **losing the ability to connect with people**. Every memory is a relationship. Every death strips one away. When there's no one left who knows you — you cease to exist.

### Memory List

**1. 아파트 경비 아저씨의 인사 (Weight 1)**
> 매일 아침 7시 52분. 아파트 현관을 나서면 경비 아저씨가 "좋은 아침이에요, 젊은이"라고 했다. 이름은 몰랐다. 아저씨도 내 이름을 몰랐을 것이다. 하지만 매일 아침, 나를 알아보고 인사해주는 사람이 한 명은 있었다.

> ...지금은 그 얼굴이 떠오르지 않는다. 매일 봤는데.

*Loss reaction:* "누군가 나를 믿어줬던 기억이 사라졌다." 영재가 흔들린다 — 그도 누군가의 믿음 때문에 이 길을 걸었으니까.

**5. 좋아한다고 말했을 때 그 사람의 표정 (Weight 2)**
> 대학교 2학년 가을. 도서관 앞 벤치. 옆에 앉은 사람에게 "나... 너 좋아해"라고 말했다.

>
> 그냥 — 그 사람이 나를 보던 그 순간의 표정만 남아있었다. 그것마저...

*Loss reaction:* 유리가 조용히 고개를 돌린다. 자기도 비슷한 기억이 있었을 것이다 — 아니, 없었을지도. 고백할 상대가 있었는지조차 모르겠다.

**6. "잘 지내?" 하고 전화하던 형(누나) (Weight 2)**
> 군대 있을 때 한 달에 한 번 전화가 왔다. "잘 지내? 밥은 잘 먹어?" 별거 아닌 통화. 3분도 안 되는.
>
> 전역 후, 자취 시작하고, 바빠지면서 전화가 줄었다. 형(누나)도 결혼하고 아이 낳고 바빠졌다. 마지막 통화가 언제였는지 모르겠다. 설? 추석? "잘 지내?" "응." "그래, 잘 지내." 그게 끝이었다.
>
> 형(누나)의 목소리가... 안 떠오른다.

*Loss reaction:* 미선의 눈이 젖는다. "내 아이들도 크면 이렇게 되는 걸까. 엄마 목소리를 잊는 걸까."

**7. 절친의 이름 (Weight 3)**
> 중학교 때부터 고등학교까지. 매일 같이 다녔다. 학교 끝나면 PC방, PC방 끝나면 편의점, 편의점 끝나면 놀이터 벤치에 앉아서 아무 말도 안 했다. "쟤네 커플이냐"고 놀림받았다.

>
> 이름이 뭐였지. 키가 나보다 컸었나. 작았었나. ...모르겠다.

*Loss reaction:* 정수가 주인공 옆에 앉는다. 아무 말 없이. 같이 앉아있는다. 그것만으로 주인공의 눈이 붉어진다 — 왜인지는 기억나지 않지만.

**8. 아버지의 등 (Weight 3)**
> 다섯 살? 여섯 살? 아빠 등에 업혀있었다. 밤이었다. 어디를 가고 있었는지 모르겠다. 아빠 등에서 땀 냄새가 났다. 등이 넓었다. 따뜻했다. 흔들리는 걸음에 맞춰 잠이 들었다.

>
> 아빠 등이... 아빠 얼굴이... 기억나지 않는다.

*Loss reaction:* 정수가 등을 돌린다. 손이 떨린다. 헬멧 안쪽의 아들 사진을 만진다. 태현이 국을 내밀지만, 정수는 먹지 않는다. 이 날만.

**9. 어머니의 목소리 (Weight 4)**

>

### Memory Loss Design Philosophy

Every memory is a **사람**. The protagonist didn't burn out from overwork — they burned out from **disconnection**. They stopped answering calls. Stopped meeting people. Stopped caring. The Abyss didn't break them. They were already broken. The Abyss just put a number on it.

The irony: in the Abyss, they form NEW relationships (companions). They're learning to connect again — in a city of the dead. And every death threatens to erase both the old connections AND the new ones.

The ultimate question the game poses through this system: **"Is it worth connecting with people, knowing you'll lose them?"**

## 1. Soyeon (소연) — District A: The Silent Mansions

**Korean Issue: 언론 탄압 / 공익신고자 보복**

### Death — The Article That Never Ran

Friday, 11:47 PM. Soyeon's apartment.

The article was done. Three months of investigation — hidden camera footage, internal documents, former employees who agreed to testify. A construction conglomerate had been falsifying safety inspection reports for 12 years. Three workers had died in the last 18 months. The most recent: a man who fell from the 42nd floor because his safety harness was expired.

She clicked "Send" to her editor. Then waited.

Her phone rang at 1 AM.

"Soyeon-ah. I got it. It's... it's good. Too good." A long pause. "Legal called. They said the company threatened to pull all advertising. That's 40% of our revenue."

"So what? We run it. That's what we do."

"...I'm pulling the series. I'm sorry."

She hung up. Stared at the screen. Then opened her personal blog, copied the entire article, and clicked "Publish."

At 4 AM, there was a knock on her door. She didn't answer. But the door opened anyway.

The police found her the next morning. Official ruling: suicide. Her laptop's hard drive had been reformatted. The blog post was already taken down — DMCA claim from the conglomerate's legal team.

No note. No struggle. No article.

Just silence.

### First Encounter — Whisper Market

The protagonist enters the Whisper Market — a narrow alley where all trade happens through sign language and written notes. Prices are gestured. Words are contraband.

In the corner, a woman with short hair is handing folded notes to passersby. Most ignore her. One reads the note, then tears it up, terrified.

A high-pitched whine. Censorship drones descend from above the rooftops, red lights scanning. They converge on the woman.

She runs. Fast, practiced — she's done this before. But three drones block the alley exit. She's cornered.

She turns around and sees the protagonist. Shoves a note into their hand:

*"이 종이를 삼켜. 안 그러면 둘 다 잡혀."*

[Choice]
- [Body DC 6] Grab a market stall and throw it at the drones → drones scatter, vendors scream silently
- [Sense DC 5] Clap hands loudly → drones redirect to the noise source, Soyeon runs
- [Bond DC 7] Stand between Soyeon and the drones and don't move → drones scan the protagonist (Code: NULL — unprocessable), error out

After escape, a quiet rooftop. Soyeon writes on her notebook and holds it up:

*"고마워. 이름은?"*

"...I don't remember."

She stares. Then writes:

*"이름 없는 사람이 이름 없는 기자를 도왔네."*

She pauses. Writes again, slower:

*"같이 다녀도 돼? 혼자 다니기엔 여기가 너무 조용해."*

### Key Arc Scene — The Connection to Jeongsu

If both Soyeon and Jeongsu are in the party, this scene triggers when the group rests at Platform 0 after clearing B-district:

Soyeon is writing in her notebook. Jeongsu sits across from her, repairing a pipe. Neither has spoken (or written) to the other much — they're both quiet people.

Soyeon glances at Jeongsu's helmet. The crack. She writes a note and slides it across the ground to him.

*"그 안전모, 어디 회사 거예요?"*

Jeongsu reads it. Pauses. "...한양건설."

Soyeon's pen stops. Her hand trembles. She writes, slowly:

*"내가 폭로하려던 회사예요. 안전 점검 보고서 위조한 회사."*

Long silence. Jeongsu looks at the crack in his helmet. Looks at Soyeon. Looks at the note.

"...그 기사. 나왔으면 뭐가 달라졌을까."

Soyeon writes: *"몰라요."*

"그래도 써줘서 고마워."

Soyeon's hand shakes harder. She writes: *"늦어서 미안해요."*

Jeongsu reads it. Carefully folds the note. Opens his helmet. Places it inside, next to his son's photo.

No more words. They sit in silence. But it's a different kind of silence than before.

## 2. Junseo (준서) — District A: The Silent Mansions

**Korean Issue: 취업난 / N수생 / 자기검열 문화**

### Death — Attempt 47

Gosiwon (고시원), Room 304. 1.5 pyeong. A desk, a bed, a lamp. No window.

Junseo opened the exam results page. Password. Loading.

**"불합격"**

Third year. Third failure. Same two characters.

His phone buzzed. Mom.

"준서야, 결과 나왔어?"

He stared at the screen. At the phone. At the screen.

"...아직 안 나왔어요."

"아, 그래? 나오면 바로 전화해! 아빠도 기다리고 있어. 이번엔 될 거야. 우리 준서 열심히 했잖아."

"네. 나오면 바로 할게요."

He hung up. Put the phone face-down. Opened Instagram. His high school classmate had posted: "9급 합격!! 3년 만에 ㅠㅠ 감사합니다!!" 847 likes. His college roommate: a photo from a company dinner, laughing. His ex: a selfie with someone new.

He closed Instagram. Opened the exam prep site. Started reviewing wrong answers. As he always did.

At 3 AM, he stopped. The answers were blurring. Not from tears — from something emptier than tears. He hadn't cried in two years. Crying required the belief that something should be different.

He took the sleeping pills. Not dramatically. Not desperately. Just — practically. The way he'd approached everything for three years. Methodically. Quietly.

The gosiwon owner found him two days later. The desk lamp was still on. On the desk: a pencil, a half-solved practice exam, and an unpaid phone bill from his mother's number.

He never called back.

### First Encounter — The Infinite Exam Hall

Behind A-district's Central Archive, the protagonist finds a hidden classroom. Hundreds of ghost students sit at desks, filling in answer sheets. Most are hollow — processed, mechanical, pencils moving without thought.

One student is different. He erases an answer. Writes it again. Erases. Writes. Erases. His pencil is worn to a nub but keeps going.

The protagonist approaches.

"What exam is this?"

The student doesn't look up. "5급. Or 7급. Or 9급. I stopped checking."

"How many times have you taken it?"

"..." He glances at the corner of his answer sheet. Stamped in red: ATTEMPT 47. "...Does it matter?"

[Choice]
- [Talk] "You should stop." → "Why? What else would I do? This is all I know."
- [Take the pencil from his hand] → He freezes. No one has interrupted the cycle. Ever.

If the protagonist takes the pencil:

"What are you— Give it back. I need to finish. If I don't finish I'll fail and if I fail then I—"

"The exam is fake. You're dead."

The room goes quiet. The other ghost students keep writing. Only Junseo has stopped.

"...I know." Very quiet. "I knew the whole time. But if I stop taking the test... what else am I?"

[Choice]
- "You're you." → He looks up. His eyes are red but dry — he forgot how to cry. "Nobody's ever said that. Not without adding 'when you pass.'"
- "I don't know. Let's find out." → He stares at the protagonist. At the pencil in their hand. At the classroom. He stands. His legs shake — first time standing in years. "...Okay."

He drops the answer sheet. It dissolves before hitting the floor.

"I'm Junseo. I'm... was 23. I don't know what I am now. But I'm not an examinee anymore."

### Key Arc Scene — "Stop Apologizing"

During a combat encounter in C-district, the group is stuck. Two choices, both bad. Minjun can't figure out the pattern. Soyeon's intel is incomplete. Jeongsu is ready to tank, but the enemy is too strong for brute force.

Junseo, standing behind everyone, mumbles: "If we use the pipe on the left wall and create steam, the enemy's vision gets blocked, and then..."

Everyone turns to look at him.

"Sorry. That was probably stupid. Forget I—"

"No." Minjun: "Say that again."

"I... if we open the steam valve, it blocks its sensors for about 6 seconds. The thermal pattern—"

"That's exactly right." Soyeon holds up a note: *"천재적이야."*

Junseo blinks. "...I just... that's how the heating system works in gosiwon. The pipes always—"

Narae, losing patience: "FOR THE LOVE OF— STOP. SAYING. SORRY. You just saved our asses!"

Junseo goes silent. Then, barely audible: "...Actually, I think I'm right."

It's the first time he's said that sentence in his entire life. Dead or alive.

Later, alone, Junseo to the protagonist:

"I wasn't afraid of failing. I was afraid of what happens after. If I pass — I have to live a life I don't know how to live. If I fail — I'm nothing. The exam was my excuse. As long as I was 'preparing,' I didn't have to face the fact that I had no idea who I was."

Pause.

"...You know what the worst part is? The exam didn't even matter. None of it mattered. But it was the only thing anyone ever asked me about. 'Did you pass?' Not 'Are you okay?' Just 'Did you pass?'"

## 3. Minjun (민준) — District B: The Iron Offices

**Korean Issue: IT 과로사 / 스타트업 크런치 문화**

### Death — The Last Commit

Tuesday, 4:07 AM. Startup office, 14th floor.

The launch was in 19 hours. Minjun had been at his desk for 72 hours. Three cans of energy drink. Two cups of vending machine coffee. One triangle kimbap at hour 48 — he couldn't remember eating it.

His desk neighbor, Sangwoo, had left at 6 PM. As always. "See you tomorrow." Minjun had nodded, barely.

## 10. Youngjae (영재) — Terminal

**Korean Issue: 비정규직 / 열정페이 / 안전 사각지대**

### Death — Platform Edge

Youngjae got the contract position at Seoul Metro in March. 10-month contract. ₩9,620/hour. No benefits. No safety training — that was for full-time employees only. His supervisor told him on the first day: "Platform duty is easy. Just stand there and help passengers."

He loved it. He loved the uniform. He loved saying "좌측으로 이동해주세요" into the mic. He loved the name tag: YOUNGJAE — INTERN, but still, his name on a badge.

On his desk at the staff room, he'd written on a Post-it: "다음 달이면 정규직!!!" His supervisor had hinted. "You're doing well. I'll put in a good word."

August 14th, 6:47 PM. Rush hour. Platform 4, Line 2.

A man on the platform edge swayed. His eyes were unfocused. Drunk, maybe. Or exhausted. Or something else entirely.

Youngjae saw it before anyone else. The man tilted forward. Gravity took him.

Youngjae didn't think. His body moved — the same way it moved when he said "좌측으로 이동해주세요." Automatic. Instinctive.

He reached the man. Grabbed his collar. Pulled him back onto the platform.

The man fell backward onto the tiles. Safe.

Youngjae's momentum carried him forward. His foot was over the edge. He saw the headlight of the oncoming train — very bright, very fast — and thought:

"아, 정규직 전환 다음 달인데."

The station erected a memorial plaque: "Our Brave Employee, Youngjae Kim, Who Gave His Life to Save a Passenger." The same station that didn't have platform screen doors because the budget had been allocated to a new ticketing system. The same station where safety training was "full-time employees only."

The man Youngjae saved went home that night. He never learned the name of the person who saved him. He took Line 2 again the next day.
